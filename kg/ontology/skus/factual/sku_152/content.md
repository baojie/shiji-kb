## The Three Virtuous Ones of Yin (殷有三仁)

**Weizi (微子)** - The eldest son of Emperor Yi and elder half-brother of King Zhou. He repeatedly admonished King Zhou but was ignored. He consulted the Grand Music Master and Junior Music Master before leaving the court. After Zhou's fall, he surrendered to King Wu carrying sacrificial vessels, bare-chested and bound. King Wu restored his position. Later, the Duke of Zhou enfeoffed him at Song to continue the Yin sacrifices.

**Jizi (箕子)** - A relative of King Zhou. When Zhou began using ivory chopsticks, Jizi predicted this would lead to extravagance and decline. He admonished Zhou but was ignored. Rather than leaving and exposing his ruler's faults, he feigned madness and became a slave, playing the lute to express his sorrow (the composition "Jizi Cao" is attributed to him). King Wu consulted him on governance, and he presented the Hong Fan (Great Plan). King Wu enfeoffed him at Korea without requiring him to be a subject. Later, when visiting Zhou, he saw the ruins of Yin and composed the "Mai Xiu" (Wheat Ears) poem.

**Bigan (比干)** - A relative of King Zhou. Seeing Jizi's fate, he declared that a subject who does not remonstrate to the death fails the people. He spoke directly to admonish Zhou. The tyrant, having heard that sages have seven openings in their hearts, killed Bigan and examined his heart.

Confucius praised these three as the virtuous ones of Yin.
